My first insight into coding starts with a very pleasant introduction through Stanford University and their Computer Science 101 Course.

University courses I hear you say?

All the big Uni’s have very good courses available direct to your armchair these days. Harvard, Rice, Stanford etc.. they’re all at it!

This particular course with Nick Parlante had already started, and I’d some catching up to do. A very simple course, but filled in the ‘blanks’ regarding the overall picture of the internet and computers.

The whole course is downloadable and comes with a un-verified certificate. A lot of the more serious university courses though, are verified through certain visual (web-cam), identification verification etc. protocols. Look through the schools, plenty of options out there.

A nice feature too is been able to download the transcript/slides.

CS101 teaches the essential features of computers with some neat image and code exercises. CS101 is geared for people with zero prior computer experience, apart from using a browser.

Some lectures, center on experiments where you write little phrases of code to get a feel for how computers work and their limitations. There are other lectures on a wide range of computer topics: hardware, software, security, and so on (complete list below).

CS101 is not a full, professional coding class. Instead, CS101 gets at the essential ideas of computers, their powers and limitations.

For the code-writing exercises, there is a code area with a Run button that works just like in a lecture. You can edit and run your code, looking at its output at once.

Topics

  • The nature of computers and code, what they can and cannot do
  • How computer hardware works: chips, cpu, memory, disk
  • Necessary jargon: bits, bytes, megabytes, gigabytes
  • How software works: what is a program, what is “running”
  • How digital images work
  • Computer code: loops and logic
  • Big ideas: abstraction, logic, bugs
  • How structured data works
  • How the internet works: ip address, routing, ethernet, wifi
  • Computer security: viruses, trojans, and passwords
  • Analog vs. digital
  • Analog vs. digital

The course length is rated as six weeks, but I easily got through it in two weeks with two to three hours per day. I had a time frame (at the time) to complete the course to receive the certificate, but generally, the course is self paced.

I’d imagine Nick Parlante to be an awesome tutor in class and would like to see more from him. An excellent introduction to computer sciences and wetted my appetite for more. HIGHLY RECOMMEND – FREE Stanford Uni CS101
